Calculating maximum residue limits (MRLs)

Scientists from Health Canada measure and set strict limits on the amount of pesticide residue on and in the food we eat.

They do this in 2 steps:

  1. assessing the dietary risk (called a dietary risk assessment)
  2. ensuring pesticides are used properly (by setting the maximum residue limit or MRL)

Step 1: Assessing dietary risk

The scientists make sure the level of pesticide on food is low enough that there are no effects on human health. The level of pesticide must be at least 100 times lower than the “no effect” level. This is the acceptable daily intake value.

When determining a safe level, scientists pay special attention to:

  • the wide variety of foods that different people in Canada like to eat
  • foods eaten by vulnerable people such as infants, seniors, children and people who are pregnant

To assess dietary risk, they compare the acceptable daily intake value with a realistic amount of food you could eat in 1 day. This realistic amount is the potential daily intake value. The potential daily intake value (what you could eat) must be lower than the acceptable daily intake value (what is safe to eat) to meet our strict human health requirements.

The acceptable daily intake, the potential daily intake and the maximum residue limit (MRL) are all well below the requirements set by Health Canada to protect your health.

Step 2: Measuring the maximum residue limit

Health Canada uses an online tool to calculate a maximum residue limit. Pesticide regulators around the world use this tool.

The tool uses:

  • internationally accepted statistical methods
  • measured pesticide residue amounts from scientific studies

MRL is measured in parts per million (ppm). To understand how much 1 ppm is, think of 2 tablespoons in a backyard swimming pool or 1 grain of sugar in 1 baking cup.

How the scientists set MRLs

After calculating the MRL, the process to establish or set an MRL involves:

  • publishing a proposed decision
  • consulting the public on the decision
  • addressing comments and adjusting the final decision
  • legally setting the MRL in a database
  • using the MRL to monitor and protect Canada’s food supply

We repeat the whole process if an MRL needs adjusting or amending.

We can set MRLs for crop groups as well as for individual crops. A crop group is a group of plants or crops categorized based on plant structure, function and how they are grown.

Within a crop group, we identify a representative crop. It is often the biggest crop produced and/or eaten. It also usually has the highest level of residues. MRLs based on a representative crop can sometimes be set to cover off all other crops in that crop group. For a listing of crop groups, please refer to residue chemistry crop groups.

Typically, an MRL applies to raw agricultural products but it can also apply to processed foods that contain raw agricultural products. However, we would set a separate MRL if a processed food product requires a higher MRL than what is specified for its raw product.

Publishing and consulting on proposed MRL decisions

Health Canada publishes a proposed maximum residue limit document (PMRL) on the pesticides consultations page. A consultation document outlines the major findings of the evaluations and the proposed MRL decision. This includes:

  • MRLs for new pesticide active ingredients
  • MRLs for new food uses of a pesticide registered in Canada
  • amendments to existing MRLs

Final MRL decisions and when they are legally in effect

We consider all comments from the public consultation before we make a final science-based decision. We address comments received in a document linked to the published PMRL. If we don’t receive any comments, or the comments we receive don’t result in a change to the proposed MRL, the established MRL will be legally in effect as of the date it’s entered into the MRL database.

MRL monitoring and compliance

Every year, the Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) tests more than 15,000 samples of a wide variety of foods for the presence of pesticide residues. Each sample can include monitoring for more than 450 different pesticide residues, generating more than 6 million pesticide residue monitoring results.

The CFIA uses a number of monitoring activities to conduct compliance testing. These activities include:

  • targeted surveys
  • the Children’s Food Project
  • National Chemical Residue Monitoring Program

When no specific MRL is set, pesticide residues must not exceed 0.1 ppm. This is commonly known as the general MRL or default MRL and is set out in subsection B.15.002(1) of the Food and Drug Regulations.

The most recent testing shows that 97% of imported foods and 99% of domestic foods meet the established or default MRL. If the CFIA finds a food with residues greater than the MRL or the default level, it takes the appropriate regulatory action.

For more information on CFIA’s National Chemical Monitoring Residue Program, please consult the 2017 to 2018 annual report.

Adjusting or amending MRLs

We often amend MRLs, as agricultural practices change in Canada and internationally. If an MRL increases, it doesn’t mean that human health protections decreases. MRLs are a function of how a pesticide is used and not a measurement of pesticide toxicity.

When farmers or food producers use a pesticide in a different way, the maximum amount of residue that could remain on that food may change.

Examples of these types of situations include using:

  • a pesticide later in the growing season
  • more of a pesticide to deal with a new introduced pest
  • a pesticide differently because of climate change

These situations would lead to an application to amend the established MRL. MRL amendments apply to both Canadian and imported foods. Under the Pest Control Products Act when we receive an application, the scientists at Health Canada assess the risk of the pesticide. They make sure it continues to meet the requirements to protect human health.

If these requirements aren’t met, the pesticide product won’t be allowed for sale or use in Canada.

Health Canada also consults on proposed MRLs when decreasing or revoking established MRLs if new information indicates risks to human health.

MRL applications

Any stakeholder can submit an application to Health Canada to set an MRL.

For example, an application can come from:

  • provincial ministries
  • pesticide manufacturers
  • Agriculture and Agri-food Canada (AAFC)
  • fruit and vegetable growers, producers and associations

Applicants must submit a full data package including scientific studies that address each of the data requirements to support an MRL application.
