Scientific considerations

Pyriproxyfen (PRX) is an analogue of an insect juvenile hormone. It is registered in Canada for the control of insects in greenhouse food and non-food crops, indoor structure, furniture and companion animals - dogs and cats (USCs 5, 6, 20, 24 and 26). As of August 18, 2015, there are one technical, 3 manufacturing concentrate, 2 commercial and 27 domestic class end-use products registered in Canada.

The following reflects the latest assessments on Pyriproxyfen

Health Considerations

Below are the reasons why pyriproxyfen meets Health Canada’s requirements for protection of human health.

Toxicology:

There is no evidence that the pesticide causes cancer or impacts growth and development of young animals.

There is some evidence of reproductive effects in laboratory animal studies. However, these effects were seen in tests where a much higher dose was applied compared to the proper use of pyriproxyfen in Canada.

When a much higher dose was applied to laboratory animals, some toxicity effects were found in liver, kidney and the cardiovascular system. However, these effects are not expected when pyriproxyfen is used properly in Canada according to the updated label directions.

Dietary exposure:

Testing showed that the amount of pyriproxyfen that could remain on food meets the health requirements as set by Health Canada. This was seen after repeated and prolonged (chronic) exposure, and in children.

Label wording is proposed to further increase safety by preventing pyriproxyfen residues from entering drinking water sources. Specifically, to prevent recycled greenhouse water from being applied to outdoor food crops, and to prevent food from being contaminated when pyriproxyfen is sprayed indoors to control fleas and ticks.

Commercial exposures:

Applicator exposure
Greenhouse crops

For greenhouse crops, exposure is within acceptable health requirements as set by Health Canada for all crops using:

  • manually pressurized hand wand
  • mechanically pressurized handgun
  • backpack sprayer
Application indoors or on companion animals, like pets

There are no commercial products for application to pets in commercial establishments (like veterinary clinics), so it is assumed that workers use domestic-class products. Exposure is within acceptable health requirements as set by Health Canada for workers treating pets with these products.

When used indoors to surfaces to control fleas, exposure is within acceptable health requirements as set by Health Canada for all application types using a manually pressurized hand wand with basic personal protective equipment (PPE):

  • broadcast
  • perimeter/spot
  • crack-and-crevice
Post-application exposure
Only applies to greenhouse crops

For workers entering a treated site for activities such as thinning, pruning and harvesting, restricted-entry intervals (REIs) are calculated to determine the minimum length of time required before people can safely enter after application. An REI is the amount of time that must pass before the pesticide residues decline to a level where exposure to the treated greenhouse crop meets Health Canada’s human health requirement.

Post-application exposure meets the requirements for human health protection because a minimum 12 hour restricted-entry interval is set for pyriproxyfen.

Residential exposures:
Applicator exposure

Only applies to application indoors or on companion animals, like pets

These products are applied as perimeter/spot, and trigger pump sprayer product applied to an entire area (broadcast application).

Skin and inhalation exposure from using pyriproxyfen to control fleas and ticks indoors meets the requirements for protection of human health as set by Health Canada.

Skin and inhalation exposure from using pyriproxyfen pet products (dog shampoo or spot-on application to dogs and cats) also meets the requirements for protection of human health.

Aggregate exposure:

Aggregate exposure is the total exposure to a single pesticide that may occur from exposure to treated food, drinking water, in a treated home and from other non-occupational sources. It considers exposure from the different routes such as oral (food and drink), dermal (skin), and inhalation (breathing). The aggregate exposure assessment meets the human health safety requirements for all scenarios.

Aggregate assessments were conducted for the following scenarios:

Commercial use to control fleas indoors:
Post-application skin exposure to adults, children and incidental oral exposure to children, and dietary exposure for adults and children.
Domestic use to control fleas and ticks indoors:
For adults, applicator skin and inhalation exposure, post-application skin exposure, and dietary exposure.
For children, post-application skin exposure + incidental oral exposure + dietary exposure.
Domestic use on pets:
For adults, applicator skin and inhalation exposure, post-application skin exposure, and dietary exposure.
For children, post-application skin exposure, incidental oral exposure, and dietary exposure.

Environmental Considerations

Below are the reasons why pyriproxyfen meets Health Canada’s requirements for protection of the environment.

Fate and behaviour in the environment

Environmental fate provides insight to what happens to a pesticide once it enters the environment. Exposure to the environment is expected to be minimal because pyriproxyfen is used indoors.

Pyriproxyfen does not dissolve well in water (hydrolytically stable). As a non-persistent pesticide, pyriproxyfen breaks down quickly in soil and does not build up in the environment. It is not expected to leach into groundwater or accumulate in biological systems.

Toxicity in the environment

The re-evaluation has confirmed the expected minimal exposure to earthworms, birds, wild mammals, and non-target plants.

Effects on beneficial terrestrial invertebrates (bees, predatory, parasitic spiders and other non-insects) are also as expected. A label statement for greenhouse uses inform users of the potential risks to beneficial non-target invertebrates is already present on the label.

Pyriproxyfen is toxic to freshwater, estuarine, and marine invertebrates and fish. It is not expected that aquatic life would be directly exposed to pyriproxyfen because it is used indoors. The potential for indirect exposure from greenhouse process water (effluent) discharge was considered. The current label includes directions prohibiting the discharge of greenhouse process water. This would minimize the potential exposure to aquatic organisms. Updated environmental precaution/disposal statements are proposed for the label to increase consistency.

Value Considerations

Below are the reasons why pyriproxyfen meets Health Canada’s requirements for providing value for use in Canada.

Pyriproxyfen is a valuable tool for managing whiteflies in the production of greenhouse vegetable and ornamental plants. It is used at the end of a reproduction cycle, to reduce whitefly populations before the next cycle. It is not applied to greenhouse vegetables when the food is present, but when the harvest is complete. Whiteflies can affect the appearance and marketability of vegetables and ornamental plants. Maintaining high quality and visual appeal is critical to these sectors.

Pyriproxyfen is useful for managing pet pests (e.g., fleas, ticks, lice, mosquitoes and stable flies) because it works for a long time (5 months). It poses a low risk of resistance development as a juvenile hormone mimic. For these reasons, is helpful as a co-formulant with insecticides that work differently to provide immediate pest population reduction.

International use

Pyriproxyfen is currently approved for use in other Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) member countries, including the United States, the European Union, and Australia.

No decision by an OECD member country as of 11 March 2019, has been identified to prohibit all uses of pyriproxyfen for health or environmental reasons.

Pyriproxyfen is used on citrus fruit in Israel, South Africa, Spain and Italy. Pyriproxyfen is one of several insecticides used for the control of the red imported fire ant (Solenopsis invicta) in California, USA. In addition, pyriproxyfen has been considered by the World Health Organisation (WHO) for vector control under its Pesticides Evaluation Scheme. The eventual goal of vector control is meant to guide interactions between product developers/manufacturers of Pyriproxyfen and the WHO.

International Use

Canada

Pyriproxyfen was first registered for indoor structure in 1997. Since then several new use sites, including companion animals, greenhouse food and non-food, have been added to the use pattern. As of March 2, 2016, there are 7 PA1 submissions under review [4 Cat A containing PRX and a new active ingredient (dinotefuran), 1 Cat B and 2 Cat C].

United States

In the U.S., pyriproxyfen is registered as an insect growth regulator for use on various agricultural, commercial, residential and aquatic settings. It is also registered on pets and pet living quarters. The USEPA published their final work plan for registration review in February 2012 (data call-in included TOX and environment, see Appendix IV for details). The proposed registration review decision is scheduled to be published in October to December 2016.

Europe

Pyriproxyfen is currently registered in the EU member countries as an insecticide on agricultural crops. The EU published its conclusion regarding the peer review of the pesticide risk assessment in 2009. No risks of concern were identified in their review.

Pyriproxyfen is also approved by European Medicine Agency as a veterinary drug used on dogs.
