Pyriproxyfen and its uses

Pyriproxyfen is an insect growth regulator registered in Canada. It is used as a:

Commercial class pesticide

These products are restricted to commercial activities as indicated on the label. These are not available to the general public for use in and around the home.

In commercial situations, it is used to control:

  • whiteflies in greenhouse food and non-food crops
  • fleas in non-food areas of indoor structures

Domestic class pesticide

These products are for personal use in and around the home.

Domestically, it's used for:

  • fleas, mosquitoes and ticks on companion animals, like pets
  • fleas and ticks in non-food areas of indoor structures

Commercial use of Pyriproxyfen in greenhouses

  • How its used

    The commercial Pyriproxyfen product is applied as a spray to greenhouse grown:

    • Tomatoes
    • Peppers
    • Cucumbers
    • Eggplants
    • Ornamental plants

    It can be applied as a spray by certified greenhouse growers and professionals.

  • The commercial greenhouse product that contains Pyriproxyfen

    Distance; VALENT Canada Inc. (PCPA Registration #: 28414)

  • Proposed changes to the commercial greenhouse use label

    Changes for protection of human health

    • To protect mixer/loaders and applicators:
      • Updated personal protective equipment
    • To make the use directions clearer:
      • Prevent recycled greenhouse water from being applied to outdoor food crops.
      • Clarifying and/or ensuring consistency about the use directions and precautionary statements

    Due to a lack of data to assess occupational exposure, label statements are proposed:


    No longer is the application of Pyriproxyfen using handheld mist blower or handheld fogging equipment allowed

    Changes for protection of the environment

    • To make protecting the environment clearer:
      • Clarify standard label statements that Pyriproxyfen is toxic to aquatic organisms

Commercial indoor use to control fleas

  • How its used

    The commercial Pyriproxyfen product is a liquid mixed with water and applied by a low-pressure indoor sprayer. It can be applied by certified professionals, such as pest control operators in indoor spaces that do not contain food.

  • The commercial indoor product to control fleas that contains Pyriproxyfen

    NYGUARD IGR CONCENTRATE; McLaughlin Gormley King Company (PCPA Registration #: 25490)

  • Proposed changes to the commercial indoor use label

    Changes for protection of human health

    • To protect mixer/loaders and applicators:
      • Updated personal protective equipment
    • To make the use directions clearer:
      • Prevent contamination of food and food surfaces during indoor applications
      • Clarifying and/or ensuring consistency about the use directions and precautionary statements

    Changes for protection of the environment

    • To make protecting the environment clearer:
      • Clarify standard label statements that Pyriproxyfen is toxic to aquatic organisms

Domestic use in your home or on pets

  • How its used

    Pyriproxyfen products are applied inside homes to control:

    • Fleas
    • Mosquitoes
    • Ticks

    The products for use in the home are applied as a trigger pump spray or in a pressurized container. The products that are used to treat pets are applied as a shampoos or as a liquid spot-on treatment.

  • Products used in your home or on a pet that contain Pyriproxyfen

    There are many different brands of domestic products that contain Pyriproxyfen. To find them all search for “Pyriproxyfen” as the active ingredient in the Pesticide Label Search.

  • Proposed changes to the domestic labels for use in your home or on pets

    Changes for protection of human health

    • To make the use directions clearer:
      • Prevent contamination of food and food surfaces during indoor applications
      • Clarifying and/or ensuring consistency about the use directions and precautionary statements
      • Remove the retreat/repeat as necessary statements
    • Update the label directions and precautions according to PMRA Guidance Document, Structural Pest Control Products: Label Updates

    Changes for protection of the environment

    • No label changes.

    NOTE: An environmental assessment is not done on this use since Pyriproxyfen domestic products are applied in the home

Proposed regulatory decision for consultation

The results from the scientific re-evaluation shows that Pyriproxyfen meets Health Canada’s requirements for:

  • the protection of human health
  • the environment, and
  • it is needed and effective for use in Canada.
Health Considerations

The purpose of assessing the risks to human health is to define the nature of potential hazards and to determine the degree and likelihood of the risk associated with a defined exposure. The PMRA considers that pesticides may pose higher risks to certain groups of people based on differences in factors such as:

  • biology
  • gender
  • sex
  • behaviour
  • age, and
  • occupation.

The PMRA’s decisions about how pesticides can be used are based on protecting the most sensitive group. The Pest Control Products Act requires that pesticide evaluations consider more sensitive populations, including pregnant women, infants, children, women and seniors.

The assessment follows a four-step scientifically established and internationally accepted process:

  1. Hazard identification
  2. Dose–response assessment
  3. Exposure assessment, and
  4. Risk characterization

For more information on this human health risk assessment process, read the document A Framework for the Risk Assessment and Risk Management of Pest Control Products – PMRA Guidance Document.

Here is a summary of the human and animal health evidence that supports the Pyriproxyfen decision:

Toxicology and human health

There is no evidence that the pesticide causes:

  • cancer, or
  • impacts growth and development of young animals.

There is no evidence of reproductive effects in laboratory animals when the pesticide is applied at the proper label rate.

  • There were some effects when Pyriproxyfen was applied in a study at a much higher amount than is allowed in Canada.

There is no evidence that the pesticide causes toxic effects in laboratory animals when the pesticide is applied at the proper label rate.

  • There were some effects seen in the liver, kidney and cardiovascular system when Pyriproxyfen was applied in a study at a much higher amount than is allowed in Canada.

Dietary assessment

In a dietary exposure assessment, the PMRA determines how much of a pesticide residue, including residues in meat and milk, may be ingested with the daily diet. Exposure to Pyriproxyfen from potentially treated imported foods is also included in the assessment.

The amount of Pyriproxyfen that remains on food meets Health Canada’s requirements for protection of human health. This includes special assessments on:

  • Chronic dietary risk
  • Risks to vulnerable groups of people such as children 1-2 years old

To ensure the protection requirements are met, these label statements are proposed:

  • To make the use directions clearer:
    • Prevent recycled greenhouse water from being applied to outdoor food crops.
    • Prevent contamination of food and food surfaces during indoor applications

Drinking water

The amount of Pyriproxyfen in drinking water is very small, because Pyriproxyfen is only approved for use indoors or in a greenhouse.

To ensure the levels in drinking water remain low, these label statements are proposed:

  • To make the use directions clearer:
    • Prevent recycled greenhouse water from being applied to outdoor food crops.

Residential & occupational risk

Residential and occupational risk is estimated by comparing potential exposures with the most relevant endpoint from toxicology studies to calculate a margin of exposure.

If the calculated margin of exposure (MOE) is less than the margin of exposure requirement for protection of health, it does not necessarily mean that exposure will result in adverse effects, but mitigation measures will be required to maintain protection of human health.

Residential risk assessment involves estimating risks in the home to the general population, including:

  • children
  • during pesticide application, or
  • after pesticide application.

Occupational risk assessment involved estimating risks in the following workplace scenarios:

  • application to greenhouse crops
  • application to indoor structures, including homes, using the commercial indoor product to control fleas
  • application of domestic-class spot-on or shampoo products to pets by workers

The amount of Pyriproxyfen that people could be exposed to in their home or at a workplace meets Health Canada’s requirements for protection of human health.

Aggregate risk

Aggregate exposure is the total exposure to Pyriproxyfen that may occur from:

  • food
  • drinking water
  • exposure in the home
  • exposure at work

It considers exposure from the different routes such as oral (food and drink), dermal (skin), and inhalation (breathing).

The aggregate risk from multiple exposures to Pyriproxyfen meets Health Canada’s requirements for protection of human health. This includes special assessments on:

  • adult health
  • children health

The overall results from the scientific re-evaluation shows that Pyriproxyfen still meets Health Canada’s requirements for the protection of human health.

Environmental considerations

The purpose of the environmental risk assessment is to determine whether adverse effects from the use of pesticides may occur to organisms and to the natural environment itself. Similar to the process for assessing the risks to human health, the environmental risk assessment looks at:

Exposure (how the pesticide moves in the environment)

  • Where the possibility for the pesticide to move into sensitive environmental compartments where the pesticide is used. The areas we consider include:
  • groundwater
  • lakes and rivers
  • atmospheric transport and deposition into remote regions, such as the Arctic.

Hazard (toxic effects on organisms)

  • Where the effects on a large number of plants and animals are assessed. These assessments include considering effects on biodiversity and the food chain.

Characterize the risks posed by pesticides

  • Where the information on environmental exposure and effects are integrated and compared to identify which, if any, organisms or environmental compartments may be at risk.

For more information on the environmental risk assessment process, read the document A Framework for the Risk Assessment and Risk Management of Pest Control Products – PMRA Guidance Document.

Here is a summary of the environmental evidence that supports the Pyriproxyfen decision:

Exposure to the environment is expected to be minimal because Pyriproxyfen is used indoors.

  • Pyriproxyfen does not dissolve well in water.
  • It is classified as non-persistent in the environment.
  • It is not expected to leach into the groundwater or accumulate in the environment.

Adverse effects (or toxicity) to aquatic animals such as fish and insects happen when they are exposed to Pyriproxyfen. However, it is not expected that aquatic animals will be directly exposed to Pyriproxyfen because it is used indoors.

To make protecting the environment clearer:

  • Clarify standard label statements that Pyriproxyfen is toxic to aquatic organisms

Adverse effects (or toxicity) to terrestrial insects and arachnids happens when they are exposed to Pyriproxyfen. This is an important consideration because beneficial insects such as pollinators are used indoors in greenhouses.

  • Label statements are already present to provide information on the potential risks to beneficial insects and arachnids

No adverse effects (or toxicity) are expected to affect terrestrial animals that are mainly found outdoors. This is because Pyriproxyfen is only used indoors and outdoor organisms such as earthworms, birds, wild mammals and terrestrial plants are not expected to be exposed to Pyriproxyfen.

The overall results from the scientific re-evaluation shows that Pyriproxyfen still meets Health Canada’s requirements for the protection of the environment.

Efficacy in Canada considerations

Efficacy of Pyriproxyfen is measured by how the product is needed and if it is effective to use in Canada. Health Canada looks at how the product “do what it claims to do” by the applicant. This assessment is based on different types of information including:

  • results from field studies
  • use history information including information from other countries where the product may be currently registered
  • published papers
  • scientific rationales.

For more information on the efficacy assessment process, read the document A Framework for the Risk Assessment and Risk Management of Pest Control Products – PMRA Guidance Document.

Pyriproxyfen is a valuable tool for managing whiteflies in the production of greenhouse vegetable and ornamental plants. It is used at the end of a reproduction cycle, to reduce whitefly populations before the next cycle. It is not applied to greenhouse vegetables when the food is present, but when the harvest is complete.

  • Whiteflies can affect the appearance and marketability of vegetables and ornamental plants.

Pyriproxyfen is useful for managing pet pests (e.g., fleas, ticks, mosquitoes, lice and certain mites). It provides control of these pests for up to five months at a time.

  • Fleas, ticks and mosquitoes can spread diseases and parasites
  • Lice and walking dandruff mites can cause irritation in pets

The overall results from the scientific re-evaluation shows that Pyriproxyfen is needed and effective for use in Canada. It still meets Health Canada’s requirements for efficacy.

International use comparisons and considerations

Pyriproxyfen is currently approved for use in other Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) member countries including:

  • United States
  • European Union
  • Australia

Pyriproxyfen is also used:

  • On citrus fruit in Israel, South Africa, Spain and Italy.
  • To control the red imported fire ant (Solenopsis invicta) in California, USA.
  • For vector control under its Pesticides Evaluation Scheme by the World Health Organisation (WHO).
  • In Brazil to control larva of mosquitos that carried the Zika virus. The Zika virus can affect human brain development. As part of the re-evaluation, Health Canada evaluated studies that were conducted in Brazil on the effects of Pyriproxyfen in drinking water. The assessment concluded that Pyriproxyfen did not contribute to any effects on human brain development.

    • The reason for why Pyriproxyfen is being re-evaluated

    Under the authority of the Pest Control Products Act, all registered pesticides must be re-evaluated by scientists at Health Canada’s Pest Management Regulatory Agency (PMRA). This ensures that they continue to meet current health and environmental requirements and continue to have value. Health Canada applies modern, evidence-based scientific approaches to assess whether pesticides proposed for registration meet Health Canada’s requirements for protection of human health and the environment, and have value.
